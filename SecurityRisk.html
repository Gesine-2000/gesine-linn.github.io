Unit 1: An Introduction to Security and Risk Management

Uncertainty vs Risk:

Risk is defined as a situation where the outcomes and the probability of the outcome can be estimated, for example by past events or data. In comparison, uncertainty is defined as a situation where the outcome cannot be determined and is therefore unknown.

Risk of Digitalization in Business Models:

To give an example, the digitalisation of a business is used. Digitalisation can lead to new uncertainty in operations, as it involves different business processes as in traditional business models. However, those uncertainties can be traced and calculated and afterwards managed as potential impacts. Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) highlight how digitalization in traditional businesses requires careful risk assessment to mitigate these challenges effectively. The case study explores how traditional businesses must adapt their risk management strategies to address digitalisation threats.

Different Risk Definitions:

The term risk is defined differently in various academical references. According to the ISO 31000 standard risk is defined as the "effect of uncertainty on objectives," emphasizing both positive and negative outcomes. PICA (Probabilistic Impact Cyber Assessment) assesses risks in terms of their likelihood and impact on cybersecurity. The Open FAIR framework quantifies risk by evaluating threats, vulnerabilities, and impacts, while OCTAVE focuses on business risks through self-assessment.

Case Study:

In their study, Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) provide a methodology for risk assessment during digitalisation, highlighting the importance of identifying and managing these risks. By applying frameworks like ISO 31000, PICA, Open FAIR, and OCTAVE, organisations can better navigate the risks of digital transformation, ensuring competitiveness while safeguarding operations.

Unit 2: Users, Assessments and the Risk Management Process

Qualitative Approach

The authors used qualitative methods to deeply explore user participation in risk management. Through interviews with stakeholders, they gathered detailed insights into how user involvement impacts risk management processes and outcomes. This approach allowed for the identification of nuanced factors and themes that may not be evident in quantitative data.

Quantitative Approach

To validate and generalize their qualitative findings, the authors employed quantitative methods. A survey was conducted with a larger sample size, and statistical analysis was used to test the hypotheses derived from the qualitative phase. This provided a broader perspective and quantified the relationship between user participation and the effectiveness of risk management.

Benefits of Each Approach

  • Qualitative Approach: Offers an in-depth understanding of user interactions with risk management, helps develop hypotheses, and identifies subtle factors.
  • Quantitative Approach: Enables large-scale hypothesis testing, quantifies relationships, and provides statistical evidence to support or refute qualitative findings.

Impact of Lack of User Access on Risk Assessment

The lack of user access can negatively affect risk assessments by limiting the identification of risks and the development of effective mitigation strategies. Without user input, the richness and accuracy of qualitative methods may be diminished, but quantitative methods using existing data can still be applied. Mitigation strategies include engaging indirect user representatives and leveraging secondary data.

Conclusion

The authors successfully integrated qualitative and quantitative approaches to provide a comprehensive analysis of user participation in risk management. Their findings emphasize the critical role of user involvement in enhancing risk identification, assessment, and mitigation, while also highlighting adaptive strategies for situations where user access is limited.

ISO 31000:

  • international standard
  • provides guidelines for risk management
  • emphasizes a structured, comprehensive approach
  • applicable to any business, regardless of size or industry
  • covers risk identification, assessment, and mitigation
  • aims to improve decision-making and achieve individual goals

The Open Group Framework (TOGAF):

TOGAF is a widely used enterprise architecture framework that guides organizations in designing, planning, implementing, and governing enterprise information architecture. It emphasizes alignment between IT and business goals, providing tools and methods for effective architecture development. TOGAF helps organizations manage complex technological changes while ensuring consistency and quality across processes.

FAIR Method (Factor Analysis of Information Risk):

FAIR is a quantitative risk analysis model that helps organizations understand, analyze, and measure information security risks. It focuses on providing a clear, data-driven view of risks by quantifying factors such as threat frequency and impact. FAIR enables organizations to prioritize security investments by translating risks into financial terms, improving decision-making and resource allocation.

Unit 3: Introduction to Threat Modelling and Management

Threats vs. Vulnerabilities
Threats and vulnerabilities are foundational concepts in cybersecurity. A threat is any circumstance or event with the potential to cause harm to a system or organization, such as a cyberattack or natural disaster. A vulnerability, on the other hand, is a weakness in a system that can be exploited by a threat. Effective risk management involves identifying vulnerabilities and mitigating potential threats to reduce the overall risk (Whitman & Mattord, 2019).

The Threat Modelling Manifesto
  • Set of guiding principles aimed at improving the practice of threat modelling
  • Developed by a group of security experts
  • Emphasizes the importance of understanding the system
  • Proactive approach to security
  • Iterative threat modelling throughout the software development lifecycle (Shostack, 2020)
STRIDE & DREAD
  • Developed by Microsoft and used in threat modelling
  • STRIDE categorizes threats into six types
  • Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege
  • DREAD evaluates threats based on five factors
  • Damage potential, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected users, and Discoverability (Herman, 2018)
CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System)
  • Common Vulnerability Scoring System
  • Rating the severity of security vulnerabilities
  • Range from 0 to 10 based on factors such as exploitability, impact, and complexity
  • Ensuring that the most critical vulnerabilities are addressed first (Mell et al., 2007)
Attack Trees
  • Conceptual tool used to analyze the security of systems
  • Represents potential attack scenarios as a tree structure
  • Attack trees help in understanding the paths an attacker might take and identifying where defenses should be strengthened (Schneier, 1999)
OCTAVE Methodology
  • Operationally Critical Threat, Asset, and Vulnerability Evaluation
  • Emphasizes a self-directed evaluation process
  • Focuses on the assets, threats, and vulnerabilities specific to the organization (Alberts & Dorofee, 2003)
QTMM (Quantitative Threat Modelling Methodology)
  • Combines qualitative and quantitative methods
  • Assesses threats by assigning numerical values to various risk factors
  • Particularly useful in complex environments (Xiong & Lagerström, 2019)
PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis)
  • PASTA is a risk-centric threat modelling methodology
  • Aligns business objectives with technical requirements
  • Seven stages
  • Provides a comprehensive understanding of threats and vulnerabilities
  • Enabling organizations to make informed decisions about security investments (UcedaVelez & Morana, 2015)
OWASP (Open Web Application Security Project)
  • OWASP is a nonprofit organization dedicated to improving software security
  • Provides free tools, resources, and guidelines
  • OWASP Top Ten, which lists the most critical web application security risks
  • Cornerstone of modern web security practices (OWASP, 2021)

NIST Overview
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) is a U.S. federal agency that develops and promotes standards and guidelines to enhance the security and resilience of the nation’s cybersecurity infrastructure. One of its most widely recognized contributions is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF), which provides a risk-based approach to managing cybersecurity risks, widely used across various industries to improve their security postures.

Positive Points of NIST
  • Comprehensive and flexible nature
  • Adaptable to organizations of all sizes and across different sectors
  • Helps express cybersecurity risk both internally and externally
  • Aligns cybersecurity efforts with business objectives
  • Makes it a practical tool for decision-makers
Negative Points of NIST
  • Can be overwhelming for smaller organizations
  • Requires a significant investment of time and expertise
  • Flexibility can also lead to inconsistent adoption and application

Unit 4: Application of Threat Modelling and Management Techniques

Shostack (2018, Chapters 3-5) delves into the practical aspects of threat modelling, emphasizing the importance of understanding system components, potential threats, and the design of security controls. The chapters highlight the application of STRIDE, the integration of attack trees, and the need for continuous updates to threat models as systems evolve.

Spring et al. (2021) builds on foundational cybersecurity concepts, exploring more advanced threat intelligence and the role of causality in understanding cyber incidents. The book critiques traditional risk assessment frameworks like CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System), pointing out their limitations in predicting real-world impacts and their failure to account for complex, multi-faceted threats. It highlights the need for more dynamic and context-aware assessment methods.

Pia (Practical Impact Analysis) offers a more nuanced approach to assessing risks, focusing on the real-world implications of vulnerabilities rather than just theoretical scores. The critiques of CVSS mentioned by Spring et al. are further supported by Pia, which highlights the system's shortcomings in capturing the true scope and severity of security risks. The book argues for a more holistic approach to vulnerability management that considers causality and the broader impact on systems.

Unit 5: An Introduction to Security and Risk Standards in Industry and the Enterprise

GDPR, PCI-DSS, ISO 27000, and NIST: Data Protection and Security

In the digital age, protecting sensitive information is crucial. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI-DSS), ISO 27000, and the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) framework are key frameworks that guide organizations in safeguarding data.

GDPR (Barafort et al., 2018)
  • Protecting Personal Data
  • Enforced in May 2018
  • Protecting the personal data of EU citizens
  • Requires organizations to obtain explicit consent, ensure data minimization, and provide individuals with rights to access and erase their data
PCI-DSS (Kirvan, 2021)
  • Securing Payment Card Information
  • Standard focused on safeguarding payment data
  • Applies to any organization handling card transactions
  • Mandates measures like encryption and access control
  • Reduces the risk of data breaches and helps build customer trust
ISO 27000 (Barafort et al., 2018)
  • Globally recognized framework for managing information security risks
  • Systematic approach to securing sensitive information
  • Aligned with GDPR and PCI-DSS

In summary, integrating GDPR, PCI-DSS, ISO 27000, and NIST into an organization's security strategy is essential for compliance and data protection.

Unit 6: The Practical Implications of Security and Risk Standards

Businesses today must adapt to various data protection regulations, such as GDPR, PCI-DSS, and HIPAA, to secure sensitive information. Each framework focuses on specific industries and data types.

HIPAA: Safeguarding Health Information

In the U.S., the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) mandates protecting personal health information (PHI). Covered entities must secure electronic PHI (ePHI) through encryption, access controls, and breach notification procedures. Violations can result in severe penalties (HIPAA, 2020).

Mitigations for Compliance

  • GDPR: Appoint a responsible DPO, perform regular internal and external audits, and integrate privacy into data handling processes.
  • PCI-DSS: Encrypt payment data, implement robust access controls, and conduct regular vulnerability scans.
  • HIPAA: Train employees on data protection, implement encryption and establish breach notification protocols.
